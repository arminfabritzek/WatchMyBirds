Native Hedgerows: 600 Species in a Single Row of Shrubs

A mature native hedgerow is not just a property boundary. It is a corridor, a larder, a nesting site, and a highway for wildlife. A single 100-metre hawthorn hedge can support over 600 species of insects, birds, mammals, and fungi — more than most manicured gardens will ever see.

What Makes a Hedgerow Alive

The key difference between a dead fence and a living hedge is structure. A wildlife hedgerow has thorny species at the core — Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna), Blackthorn (Prunus spinosa), and Dog Rose (Rosa canina) — that create impenetrable nesting cover for birds like Whitethroats, Dunnocks, and Yellowhammers. The thorns keep predators out while the dense interior stays dry and sheltered.

A single old Hawthorn in flower produces enough nectar to support over 300 insect species. Its autumn berries (haws) are a critical fuel stop for migrating Redwings and Fieldfares arriving from Scandinavia in October.

The Food Calendar

A well-mixed hedge provides food year-round. Blackthorn flowers first in March (before leaves appear), feeding early bees. Hawthorn blooms in May. Elder flowers in June. Then come the fruits: sloes in September, haws in October, rosehips and elderberries through November. Each species fills a different window. Remove one, and a gap opens in the food chain.

The Nesting Calendar

Wrens build their dome nests deep inside hedges from late March. Blackbirds and Song Thrushes prefer the fork of a mature hedge shrub. Long-tailed Tits weave their elastic, lichen-covered nests in the upper canopy. Hedge Sparrows (Dunnocks) nest low, often just 30 cm above ground. Each species uses a different height and density layer — which is why a thick, tall hedge supports far more nesting pairs than a trimmed one.

The Insect Layer

Leaf litter at the hedge base is where moth caterpillars pupate, ground beetles hunt slugs, and hedgehogs forage. The bark of older stems hosts lichen and moss — tiny ecosystems in themselves that feed micro-moths and springtails. Hoverflies lay eggs on aphid-covered shoots. Without this insect layer, there would be no food for nesting birds in spring.

Which Species to Plant

A good native hedge in Central Europe uses 5–7 species. Here are the essential ones:

Species Role Flowers Fruit
Hawthorn (Crataegus) Core structure, nesting May Oct–Nov (haws)
Blackthorn (Prunus spinosa) Early nectar, dense thorns March Sep–Oct (sloes)
Dog Rose (Rosa canina) Climbing cover, hips for birds June Oct–Dec (rosehips)
Elder (Sambucus nigra) Fast filler, summer nectar June Aug–Sep (elderberries)
Field Maple (Acer campestre) Height, autumn colour, aphid host May Seeds (Oct)
Hazel (Corylus avellana) Catkins for early bees, nuts for Dormice Feb Sep–Oct (hazelnuts)
Spindle (Euonymus europaeus) Autumn fruit, caterpillar host May Oct (pink capsules)

How to Manage — Less is More

  • Never cut all sides in one year. Trim one side this year, the other next year. This always leaves fruit and nesting cover.
  • Cut in late winter (January–February), after birds have eaten the berries and before nesting begins in March.
  • Use hand tools on small hedges. Flail mowers shatter stems and create entry points for disease. Sécateurs leave clean cuts that heal faster.
  • Let some sections grow tall. Standard trees (especially Oak or Ash) emerging above the hedgeline are used by Kestrels as hunting perches and by Barn Owls as roost sites.
  • Leave the base untidy. Dead wood, leaf litter, and tussocky grass at the hedge foot are where most invertebrate life lives. Never "clean up" a hedge base.

The Network Effect

Hedgerows work best when they connect to other hedgerows, woodlands, or wild patches. A continuous hedge network lets Dormice travel between feeding areas without crossing open ground. It allows ground beetles to colonise new gardens. It gives bats — like the Common Pipistrelle — a navigational corridor between roost and hunting ground. Isolated hedges still support life, but connected hedges support ecosystems.

In England, the total length of hedgerows exceeds the total length of roads. In Germany, hedge loss since 1950 is estimated at 50–80% in intensively farmed regions. Every new metre of native hedge is a small act of restoration.
